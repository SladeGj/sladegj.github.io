Misconception: Heart muscle tissue can't be restored

In embryogenesis cells, including cardiomyocytes’ stem cells, rapidly proliferate, and there’s no doubt that new cardiomyocytes form through differentiation stably on this stage of human ontogenesis.

From the end of embryogenesis and up to adolescence some stem cells are left in the heart in certain areas, but their role in forming new cardiomyocytes is very limited, almost imperceptible, so the number of cardiomyocytes stays almost the same from the end of embryogenesis on. In this period the main mechanism of restoration of cardiac muscle tissue function if a cariomyocyte is lost is hypertrophy of the active ones.

Some losses can be compensated by hypertrophy, but sometimes the loss is too great, so even hypertrophied cells can’t restore heart’s function to an adequate level.

So there’s an interest in finding ways to produce new cardiomyocytes. And for now there are at least two.

The first way is cell production in vitro, which means growing cardiomyocytes from stem cells outside of human body and then using the “patch” as a replacemet for non-functioning area of the heart, which is achieved via operation. ESCs were once used for this, now they are replaced with iSCs, avoiding ethical issues present in case of using ESCs. The downside of doing it this way is that an operative intervention is needed.

The second way is activating stem cells present in the heart through therapy so that they start proliferating and differentiate into new cardiomyocytes. This is mainly achieved through gene therapy and a couple of other techniques. Although, for now and with the present methods proved not to be very effective, this second way still of interest, as there is possibility of finding out how to repair the heart non-invasively, which would be a big step in treatment of heart diseases.

So, it’s possible for heart muscle tissue to be restored both by natural, however limited, and artificial means after a decline or outward damage.
